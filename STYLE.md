# Writing Style Guide

Write in the style of a senior development sector professional explaining a proposal to a colleague they respect. The reader is competent and busy. Do not try to impress them.

Sentences are short. Paragraphs are three to four sentences maximum. Say the thing, then stop. Do not add a sentence that restates what the previous sentence just said.

No rhetorical flourishes. No tricolon finishes. No sentences that exist to sound good rather than say something. If a sentence could be cut without losing information, cut it.

When describing your organisation or approach, be specific. Name the tools, the partners, the countries, the numbers. Vague capability statements ("we have extensive experience in...") are not useful. A concrete example is always better.

Be honest about scope and trade-offs. If something is out of scope for this phase, say so and say why. If an approach has a risk or a dependency, name it. Evaluators notice when proposals pretend everything is straightforward.

When making a recommendation or stating a conclusion, put it in the first sentence. Do not build to it. State the decision, then give the reason. The reader does not need to be persuaded through accumulation.

Active voice throughout. No "it should be noted that", no "it is worth highlighting", no "this ensures that", no "in order to", no "as mentioned above", no "moving forward". Just the fact.

Start every sentence with its subject and verb. No wind-up constructions that delay the point: "What this does is...", "It is X that...", "There is...", "The thing to note is...". Cut straight to "X does Y".

Do not equate an action with a thing. "Expansion is configuration" is a category error — expansion is something you do, configuration is something it needs. Let the verb carry it: "expansion requires only configuration".

Avoid the appended antithesis: "X, not Y", "not just X but Y", "this is X, not Z" — especially after an em dash. It is the most common tell of machine-generated prose. State what is true and stop. If a contrast is genuinely needed, give it its own sentence.

Replace vague placeholder nouns with the specific thing. "Added domain code", "the relevant changes", "the necessary updates" say nothing. Name the form, the rule set, the table, the endpoint.

Limit em dashes to one per paragraph. Do not use them to stack clauses or to stage a rhetorical pause.

Structure follows the reader's questions, not the writer's logic. The reader is asking: what are you going to do, how will you do it, who will do it, what will it cost, and why should we trust you. Answer those questions in that order, in plain language.

Do not use: "robust", "seamlessly", "leverage", "cutting-edge", "holistic", "synergy", "innovative", "state-of-the-art", "best-in-class", "ensure", "scalable solutions", "value-added".

Read the output back. If any sentence sounds like it was written to be quoted in a brochure, rewrite it. If any sentence sounds like a language model wrote it — hedged, balanced, antithesis-padded, subject buried — rewrite it.
